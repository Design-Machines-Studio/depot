## Systems-Informed Propaganda Strategy for Social Media

This reference adds strategic framing to the social-media skill, ensuring DM's social content follows the propaganda principles uncovered through RAG research (March 2026). It connects to the R3 (Propaganda) loop in DM's systems dynamics map.

**Load this reference when**: drafting social media content for Design Machines, planning content calendars, advising on content strategy, choosing topics for posts, or reviewing content for strategic alignment.

---

### The Narrative Insurgency Principle

DM's propaganda doesn't create a new narrative — it enters existing ones and redirects them. The audience for R3 content is **people who've never heard of co-ops** but already believe in autonomy, ownership, and flat hierarchy. Tech workers, designers, agency people, indie studio founders.

**The formula**:
1. Name a problem the audience already has (decision paralysis, founder capture, equity arguments, burnout from top-down management)
2. Show how cooperative governance solved it
3. Let the reader connect the dots

**Never**: lead with co-op ideology, explain co-ops from first principles, or assume the reader cares about the solidarity economy. They care about their own problems. Meet them there.

---

### The Insularity Trap (Smucker's Warning)

Jonathan Smucker's "Beyond the Low Plateau" warns against building infrastructure for a movement that talks only to itself. The co-op world already has this problem — same 200 people at conferences, publications nobody outside reads, language that signals insider status.

**Content that only reaches existing co-op believers is a balancing loop disguised as reinforcing.** It feels like growth but it's just circulation.

**When drafting, ask**: "Would someone who's never heard of a co-op find this interesting?" If no, it's R2 (ecosystem maintenance), not R3 (propaganda). Both are fine — but know which one you're doing.

### Channel Strategy Per Platform

| Platform | Propaganda Role | Target Audience | Content Approach |
|----------|----------------|-----------------|------------------|
| **LinkedIn** | Primary R3 channel | Tech workers, designers, agency people, entrepreneurs who've never considered co-ops | Enter through governance/workplace problems they recognize. One strong insight per post. Never preach co-ops — let the solution reveal itself. |
| **Instagram** | Visual propaganda | Culture-adjacent audience discovering through Explore/Reels | Poster-energy visuals that name power dynamics. Satirical infographics about workplace absurdity. The visual IS the argument. |
| **Bluesky** | Maker credibility | Journalists, academics, developers who amplify ideas into mainstream | Build-in-public content that demonstrates co-op tech works. Assembly progress, Live Wires experiments. Proof through doing, not arguing. |
| **Mastodon** | Community trust | EU-heavy technologists and co-op adjacent thinkers | Workshop-door-open authenticity. Process sharing, genuine questions, community participation. Earns amplification through being a real community member. |

### Content Topics That Follow the Formula

**Enter through the problem** (what the audience already feels):
- "Why does every team meeting end with no decisions?" → consent-based governance
- "Your co-founder just took 60% equity. Now what?" → worker-member equity structures
- "Flat hierarchy sounds great until nobody's accountable" → governance roles without hierarchy
- "You built the company. They own the company. Why?" → worker ownership models
- "Every 'unlimited PTO' policy is a lie" → co-op transparency and accountability
- "The equity cliff nobody talks about" → patronage-based allocation vs cliff vesting

**Don't lead with** (co-op insider language that triggers the insularity trap):
- "Why cooperatives matter"
- "The seven cooperative principles"
- "Solidarity economy solutions"
- "Democratic ownership explained"

These are fine for co-op publications (R2). They're poison for propaganda (R3) because they filter out the exact audience you need to reach.

---

### Tipping Point Sequencing

R3 content has two phases:

**Phase 1 (Now — Before R1's First Turn)**: Build-in-public content about the process. "We're building governance software for worker co-ops" is interesting because of the making, not the product. This is primarily Bluesky and Mastodon content — maker communities, curious technologists.

**Phase 2 (After TACO Ships)**: Proof-based propaganda. "Here's how a real co-op makes decisions using the system we built." This unlocks LinkedIn and Instagram as primary R3 channels because you have something concrete to point at. The product becomes the argument.

**Don't skip to Phase 2 language before you have Phase 2 evidence.** Build-in-public is the bridge.

### Wright's Eroding Strategy (Positioning Language)

Erik Olin Wright's "eroding capitalism" framing gives DM its positioning language: cooperatives don't need to overthrow anything — they just need to be better at specific things until the cooperative sector grows enough to shift norms.

**For social content, this means**: Never argue *against* conventional business structures. Make co-ops look so competent, so well-governed, so obviously functional that the comparison makes itself. The propaganda isn't "capitalism is broken" — it's "look how well this works."
