# Cornerstone Blocks — The Enforcement Angle

Cornerstone content blocks for "bylaws become operational, not aspirational." **Adapt, don't drop in.** The pitch: every other governance tool treats bylaws as reference docs. Assembly enforces them as operational defaults. Member-facing UI cannot accidentally pass a special resolution with the wrong threshold.

The principle stays constant. The examples rotate. Avoid the word *compliance*. It is on the SaaS-pitch avoid list. Use *operationalizing the bylaws*.

## The concrete examples (rotate; use 2-3 per post)

- **Special resolutions** require 2/3 of votes cast (BC Co-op Act s.1 definition; abstentions don't count against). Assembly enforces the threshold. You cannot accidentally pass a special resolution by simple majority.
- **AGMs must occur within 15 months** of the prior AGM. Assembly tracks the prior date and surfaces the deadline 90 days out.
- **Director-change filings** are due to BC Registries within 14 days. Late filings carry fines up to $5,000 per incident. Assembly drafts the filing automatically when the board record updates.
- **Block in consent decision-making** requires a written reason (this is sociocracy practice, not BC Act). Assembly enforces the reason field.
- **New member approvals** follow each co-op's actual bylaws (probationary period, board approval, AGM vote, whichever your rules describe). Assembly reads the config and enforces the right path.

## LinkedIn long-form (~150-300 words)

Last week a 12-member co-op almost passed a bylaw amendment with 51% of the vote. The bylaw amendment requires 2/3 of votes cast. The room only caught it because someone happened to know the threshold by heart. That's the gap I keep seeing.

Most governance tools treat bylaws as PDFs. The bylaws sit in a folder. Members are expected to read them. The system that runs the meeting doesn't know what's in them. The whole thing depends on someone in the room being the human keeper of the rules.

Assembly does it differently. The bylaws aren't reference material. They're configuration. The system reads each co-op's actual rules and enforces them. Special resolutions need 2/3 of votes cast? You can't accidentally pass one at 51%. AGM has to happen within 15 months of the last one? The deadline shows up 90 days out, in the dashboard, with the filing draft already started. Director changes need to file within 14 days or the co-op risks a $5,000 fine? Assembly drafts the filing the moment the board record updates.

This is the move. Bylaws stop being paperwork. The rules run the system.

The board still writes the bylaws. The lawyer still reviews them. Members still ratify them. None of that goes away. What goes away is the gap between formalization and enforcement. The gap that turns governance into homework. The gap that concentrates institutional knowledge in whoever happens to remember the threshold.

Operationalizing the bylaws is the product. Everything else follows.

## Adapt, don't drop in

The principle ("bylaws become operational") and the concrete examples stay constant. Which 2-3 examples to feature depends on the audience.

- Co-op developers and federation staff care about director filings. It's their TA pain.
- Worker co-op members care about decision thresholds and AGM timing.
- Boards in conversion care about the new-member approval flow because that's the bylaw they touch first.

Avoid pitching this as compliance. The word is on the avoid list because it imports a regulator-and-vendor relationship that doesn't fit how co-ops actually run. Use *operationalizing the bylaws*.
