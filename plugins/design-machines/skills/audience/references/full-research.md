## The 6 most important insights for Design Machines

The synthesis layer. These are the conclusions the rest of the research supports.

### 1. The channel is the strategy. Sell to developers and federations first, always.

The "sell one, it sells a bunch" instinct is correct. It matches how every successful movement infrastructure tool (Loomio, Decidim, ICA Group's bylaws templates) has reached small co-ops. Direct sales to a 12-person cleaning co-op is inefficient and misses the trust structure. Federation and developer endorsement is worth an order of magnitude more than any ad spend. Treat developer partnerships as the distribution layer, not as nice-to-have.

The developer/federation pitch is a different product from the co-op pitch. The developer cares about pipeline economics, client retention, curriculum embedding, co-branded artifacts, and whether Assembly makes their consulting engagement more valuable. The co-op cares about governance that works. Two decks, two landing pages, two sales motions. Not one.

### 2. The survival reframe is a propaganda weapon DM is not using.

Every piece of pro-co-op content should be aware of, and ideally reference, the 76%-vs-42% UK survival fact (or the France / Italy analogs). Most people in the broader business world assume co-ops are fragile, and most pitches to co-ops assume they need help to survive. Invert it. *"The business form that outlasts everyone else deserves infrastructure that does the same."*

This lives beautifully in DM's voice register. It is provocative, empirically grounded, politically useful. It is also the kind of fact the Left does not circulate enough because it has been beaten into a defensive posture. Be the ones who redeploy it.

### 3. The moat is integration, not any single feature.

Loomio is better at decisions. Decidim is better at mass participation. Notion is better at documents. A spreadsheet is better at equity until it is not. Assembly wins by being the only thing that handles proposals + meetings + members + equity + compliance + documentation as one governed-together system. Every marketing message should reinforce that. Every feature decision should defend it.

Resist the temptation to compete head-to-head on any single fixture. Live Wires competing with Tailwind on features would be a losing game. Assembly competing with Loomio on decision UX would be the same trap. Compete on the integration.

### 4. Bylaws become operational, not aspirational. This is a standalone selling line.

Every existing tool in the sector treats bylaws as reference material. Assembly treats them as rules the system enforces. Quorum thresholds, special-resolution majorities, AGM timing, director-change deadlines, block-with-mandatory-comment, new-member approval paths. These are not features. They are the product.

On the Assembly marketing site, this should be a first-fold line, not buried. On a developer-federation pitch, this is the answer to "why not just use Loomio and Notion?" Chris Galloway's April 21 feedback ("verify this is enforced in code") is a reminder that enforcement is product, not a compliance afterthought.

### 5. The "decolonizing language" move is proprietary territory.

Chris flagged it casually but it is a major opportunity. The sector is full of legal-technical vocabulary that no new co-op member can parse without a TA session. Assembly could be the first governance tool whose default language meets members where they are instead of where the 1970s co-op lawyers left them. This doubles as propaganda (accessibility is a values win) and as product differentiation (nobody else is doing it).

The plain-language glossary now lives in the council/governance plugin and is open for any co-op to adapt. It is the trust signal that gets picked up across federations.

### 6. The Estonian OÜ is a trust cost. The partnerships are the counterweight.

The one uncomfortable finding. For this specific audience, an Estonian e-residency company registration reads mildly crypto-tax-avoidant, even though it is not. Not a deal-breaker, but noticeable. The LT10 conversion credential and the active partnerships with Co-Active (Chris), Slate (Mario), and the TACO pilot are far more load-bearing for credibility than the DM registration itself. Lead with those. Keep the OÜ in the small print. Lead with the movement work.

A second credential, like being accepted into USFWC as a Cooperative Developer class member, or being a formal partner of a North American federation, would substantially shift this. Worth pursuing in 2026.
