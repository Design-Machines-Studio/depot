# Pitch: Existing worker co-ops directly

The secondary audience. The Tuesday-morning member; the small-to-mid co-op (5–30 members) that knows what governance is supposed to feel like and is currently making it work despite their tools, not because of them.

## The Tuesday morning frame

The emotional outcome to pitch: *"I own where I work, and my ownership feels real on a Tuesday."*

What that looks like:

- Logs in Tuesday morning, sees three decisions pending, one is theirs to vote on. Votes in under a minute.
- Knows what their share is worth without asking the treasurer.
- Knows when the AGM is, what's on the agenda, and what their role is. Not panicked the week before.
- Reads the minutes of last month's board meeting in five minutes.
- Brings a new hire in Tuesday afternoon and walks them through onboarding without printing anything.
- Feels democracy is ambient, not an obligation.
- Is not the person everyone else asks.

This is the emotional register the pitch lives in. Not feature lists. Not ROI math. A Tuesday morning that feels different.

## The admin debt frame

Governance equivalent of technical debt. Bylaws drift from how the co-op actually works. Equity spreadsheets only Sarah understands. Decisions get relitigated because nobody can find the last one. The Tuesday-morning member can't act without "asking Sarah." The cost compounds quietly until someone leaves and the institutional memory walks out the door.

Assembly's diagnosis: admin debt is a measurable, fixable thing. The same way you wouldn't run a production system on a spreadsheet, you shouldn't run a co-op on one.

The phrase lands because it's materialist. Debt is something you carry. Something with a balance. Something you pay down. It belongs to the labor / factory / publishing world, not the SaaS world.

## The two moats argument (member-facing version)

- **Integration:** decisions, meetings, members, equity, compliance, documentation in one place. No more "log into four tools to know what's happening."
- **Enforcement:** the bylaws actually run the system. You can't accidentally pass a special resolution with the wrong threshold. You can't miss the AGM deadline. Block requires a written reason. New-member approval follows the path your bylaws actually describe.

This is not about adding more software. It is about making the software you have stop competing with the governance you decided to do.

## The survival reframe (member version)

"You already run a more durable business form than most of the companies that pitch you software. UK co-ops survive five years 76% of the time; conventional companies, ~42%. France and Italy show the same pattern. Assembly is how you compound that lead."

Quietly delivered. Not chest-thumping. The numbers do the work.

This also calms new members nervous about whether the form is "real." It is not a sales line for veterans; it is confidence-building for the people three months in.

## What NOT to lead with

- Feature lists ("Decisions, meetings, members, equity, compliance, documentation, all in one place"). True; lands flat.
- ROI math (member co-ops don't think about ROI; they think about whether the work is sustainable).
- "Enterprise-grade" anything.
- Comparison-to-Loomio framing (Loomio is widely loved in this audience; do not punch sideways).
- "Empower your members." Members already have power. They want infrastructure, not empowerment.

## What to lead with

- The Tuesday morning frame.
- The admin debt diagnosis (quietly devastating; not preachy).
- The survival reframe ("you already last longer; this is how you compound that").
- A concrete artifact (a real proposal in their actual bylaws context).
- The plain-language defaults (show "how we're splitting this year's surplus" beside "patronage allocation subject to qualified written notice of allocation"; the contrast does the work).

## Anchor lines

- "Governance is a rhythm, not homework."
- "Inhabited interfaces, not archived snapshots."
- "Bylaws become operational, not aspirational."
- "Solidarity is the business model."
- "I own where I work, and my ownership feels real on a Tuesday."
