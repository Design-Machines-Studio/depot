# Identity Anti-Patterns

Named patterns to reject when evaluating or advising on identity design.

---

## AI-Generated Mark Tells

Signs that a mark was generated rather than designed. These patterns emerge because LLMs optimize for visual complexity and surface appeal rather than the conceptual rigor that identity design demands.

### The Gradient Shield

- **Problem:** Abstract swoosh or shield shape with a gradient fill. Often accompanied by a sans-serif wordmark in medium weight.
- **Why it fails:** Rand: a logo must work in monochrome, at any size, in any medium. Gradients are color-dependent and size-dependent -- they collapse to mud at small scales and lose their effect in single-color reproduction.
- **Fix:** Design in black first. Add color last. If the mark needs a gradient to look interesting, the underlying form is weak. Rand's seven criteria: start with distinctiveness and visibility, not decoration.

### The Initial Monogram Without Concept

- **Problem:** Two or three letters combined with no conceptual relationship beyond "these are the initials."
- **Why it fails:** Bass: "Symbolize and summarize." A monogram that merely arranges letters is typography, not identity. The letters must interact to create meaning -- a visual idea that makes the combination inevitable rather than arbitrary.
- **Fix:** Find the concept first. Then determine if a monogram serves it. The best monograms (IBM, HP, GE) work because the letter relationships create visual systems, not because someone combined initials.

### The Complexity Trap

- **Problem:** Detailed illustration presented as a logo. Multiple colors, fine lines, intricate internal detail.
- **Why it fails:** Draplin's "Twitter to billboard" test -- a mark must work at every scale from favicon to building signage. Detail disappears at small sizes. Rand's blur/distance test: a good mark is recognizable when blurred.
- **Fix:** Apply the 16x16 favicon test. Render the mark at 16 pixels square. If it's unrecognizable, simplify. Wyman's environmental wayfinding proves that marks must work at distance and speed -- simplicity isn't aesthetic preference, it's functional requirement.

### The Trendy Treatment

- **Problem:** Currently fashionable visual style applied to a mark: AI gradient blobs, outline-only logos, specific illustration trends, generative art patterns.
- **Why it fails:** Bass's logos averaged 34-year lifespans. Trends date within 3-5 years. An identity built on a trend will need replacement when the trend passes.
- **Fix:** Ask: "Could this have existed 20 years ago and still felt fresh? Will it feel fresh 20 years from now?" If the answer to either is no, the mark is trend-dependent. Timelessness comes from solving the communication problem (Rand), not from stylistic choices.

### The Meaningless Abstract

- **Problem:** Abstract geometric shapes (circles, swooshes, interlocking forms) with no connection to the organization's purpose or values.
- **Why it fails:** Rand: "A logo derives meaning from the quality of the thing it symbolizes." Glaser: meaning must be discoverable. An abstract form that could represent any company represents no company.
- **Fix:** Root the mark in something specific. Wyman's Mexico Olympics mark uses pre-Columbian geometry for cultural grounding. Draplin's marks encode regional identity. The abstraction should be OF something, not just a shape.

### The Inconsistent System

- **Problem:** A mark that looks good in one application but has no system for extending to other contexts -- no clear rules for spacing, placement, color variation, or co-branding.
- **Why it fails:** Wyman: identity is environmental and systematic. A mark that works only as a centered hero on a white background isn't an identity system -- it's a single rendering.
- **Fix:** Design the system, not just the mark. Define minimum size, clear space, color variations (full color, single color, reversed), and placement rules. The mark seeds the system (Rand), but the system must be designed alongside the mark.
